# Sympathetic Nervous System

The **sympathetic nervous system (SNS)** is one of the two classic branches of the autonomic nervous system — the **mobilization branch**, or "gas pedal." Its job is to fund metabolic effort for whatever the situation demands: not only fight-or-flight emergencies, but also exercise, focused work, excitement, and play. (Lesson 2)

## Mechanism

SNS activation arrives in two waves:

1. **Immediate (adrenaline/noradrenaline)** — a fast surge from the adrenal medulla, suited for sprints: rapid heart rate increase, bronchodilation (airways open), and a "peripheral shunt" that redirects blood from skin and gut toward the muscles.
2. **Sustained (cortisol via the HPA axis)** — a slower-onset, longer-lasting wave suited for endurance demands, raising and holding baseline arousal. (Lesson 2)

Both waves prioritize survival/action functions over maintenance functions (digestion, immune activity, tissue repair) — the opposite of the [[parasympathetic-branch]]'s anabolic role.

## Relevance to the Window of Tolerance

In [[polyvagal-theory]]'s [[evolutionary-stack|three-tier hierarchy]], the SNS sits as the middle, "reptilian" tier — older than the [[ventral-vagal-complex|Ventral Vagal system]] but more recent than the [[dorsal-vagal-complex|Dorsal Vagal system]]. Under [[no-skips-rule|Jacksonian dissolution]], it is the second circuit to take over as stress increases, and the first circuit a person must pass back *through* when recovering from [[dorsal-vagal-complex|Dorsal]] shutdown (the "thaw"). (Lesson 3)

SNS dominance with the [[vagal-brake]] released defines **Zone 2 (hyper-arousal)** in the [[three-zones]] model — but SNS activation itself is not pathological. It becomes a problem only when it runs chronically with no [[ventral-vagal-complex|Ventral]] anchor present, which is also a major driver of [[allostatic-load]]. (Lesson 4)
